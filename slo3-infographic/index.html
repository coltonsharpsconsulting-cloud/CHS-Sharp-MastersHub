Four Theories of Bilingual Development

A field guide for the secondary mathematics & computer science classroom

Colton Sharp · American Leadership Academy · Grades 9–12 · Program model: Structured English Immersion (SEI)

Multilingual learners in my Algebra, AP Calculus, and Computer Science classes can grasp the content yet stall on the academic language that carries it. These four theories explain why — and each one points to concrete moves I can make so language never hides what a student actually knows. Every panel ties a theory to classroom application, teacher advice, and a resource to go deeper.

1

Translanguaging

Ofelia García

Premise

Bilinguals don't have two separate languages — they have one integrated linguistic repertoire and draw on all of it to make meaning. Translanguaging treats a student's home language as a resource, not a deficit (García, 2013).

In the math & CS classroom

Students reason about a proof or debug code in their strongest language, then move into academic English. Comprehension can happen first; English output follows — which protects the math from the language barrier.

Advice for teachers

  • Build a translanguaging space: let students preview, discuss, and draft in their home language before producing academic English.
  • Use bilingual glossaries for technical terms (e.g., slope / pendiente, loop / bucle) and welcome first-language annotation of problems.
  • Let a student explain their thinking in Spanish, then co-construct the written English justification.

Input & Affective Filter

Stephen Krashen

Premise

Language is mostly acquired subconsciously through comprehensible input — messages just beyond a learner's current level ("i+1"). A high affective filter (anxiety, low confidence) can block that input from ever landing (Krashen, 1987).

In the math & CS classroom

Make rigorous content comprehensible — visuals, manipulatives, worked examples, gestures, live-coded demos — while keeping anxiety low so students stay open to the language around the math.

Advice for teachers

  • Front-load key vocabulary with visuals and sentence frames; pair new terms with worked examples.
  • Lower the filter: generous wait time, low-stakes participation, no cold-call shaming.
  • Scaffold the language, never the rigor — keep the grade-level concept, simplify the wording (Malone, 2012).

Social Interactionist

Vygotsky · Long

Premise

Language grows through meaningful social interaction, not grammar drills. Learners advance within their Zone of Proximal Development with support from teachers and peers, and negotiation of meaning drives acquisition (Malone, 2012).

In the math & CS classroom

Structured talk — math discourse, think-pair-share, pair programming — gives multilingual learners an authentic reason to use academic language. Classroom discourse is the engine of academic language.

Advice for teachers

  • Design talk-rich tasks with defined roles and sentence frames for academic language functions — explain, justify, compare, predict (Stanford, n.d.).
  • Teach the language of arguing and defending a solution explicitly (Sweetwater Union HSD, 2010).
  • Model academic discourse, then hand it to students through accountable-talk routines.

4

Cognitive & Academic Language

Cognitive SLA · Cummins (BICS/CALP)

Premise

Acquisition is cognitive skill-building — moving from controlled to automatic processing through meaningful practice. Crucially, conversational fluency develops faster than academic language, so a student can sound fluent yet struggle with the academic language of content (IRIS Center, n.d.; Malone, 2012).

In the math & CS classroom

Don't read conversational English as readiness for the academic English of definitions, proofs, and algorithms. Separate content difficulty from language difficulty — and teach the academic register directly.

Advice for teachers

  • Explicitly teach academic vocabulary and discourse; use graphic organizers and worked examples for repeated, meaningful practice toward automaticity.
  • Use language-function frames so students can produce the academic language, not just understand it (Sweetwater Union HSD, 2010).
  • Assess the math, not the English — give read-alouds, glossaries, and alternate ways to show mastery (IRIS Center, n.d.).
