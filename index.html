① How Students Are First Identified

Before classification, every Arizona student goes through the same legal entry process so that those who need language support are found and served (Arizona Department of Education [ADE], 2024).

#StepWhat happens
1Home Language Survey (HLS)Completed at enrollment by every family. A language other than English triggers a referral for screening.
2Placement Screener (AZELLA)Measures English proficiency in listening, speaking, reading, and writing.
3Classification & PlacementResults determine EL status and language-program placement; families are notified.
4Annual ReassessmentELs take AZELLA each year to track growth toward proficiency.
5Reclassification & MonitoringProficient students are reclassified and monitored to confirm mainstream success.

② Which Kind of EL Is This Student?

A student is identified as an EL through the process above — but "English learner" is an umbrella. Use this branching tool to reason toward the right subprofile and the supports that follow. Your answers build the logic; you can go back at any point.

③ Compare the Populations at a Glance

The same EL label covers very different students. Reading across this table is the fastest way to keep them straight.

ProfileWho they areKey needsTop supports
LTEL
Long-Term EL		 Classified EL 6+ years; usually U.S.-schooled; sounds conversationally fluent. Academic language & literacy; can be "invisible" behind oral fluency (OELA, 2024). Explicit academic vocabulary, content-area reading/writing, keep rigor high, visible reclassification goals.
RAEL
Recently Arrived		 In U.S. schools a short time (ESSA: <12 mo.); newcomer to language & school culture. Early English; a silent period is normal; cultural adjustment. Comprehensible input (visuals, gestures, realia), native-language resources, honor the silent period.
SIFE
Interrupted Education		 Recent arrival with 2+ year gaps in schooling; often below grade level in home language too. Building English and foundational literacy/numeracy; school routines. Foundational skills with grade-level content, explicit "how to do school," strong mentoring (Colorín Colorado, n.d.).
Refugee
Special Population		 Resettled after fleeing conflict/persecution; may carry trauma; often also SIFE. Safety & belonging first; simultaneous country, culture, & language adjustment. Trauma-informed routines, family/community wraparound, culturally responsive teaching (Colorín Colorado, n.d.).

④ What Proficiency Data Suggests

AZELLA reports growth across four domains. Read the profile, not just the overall number — the same score can mean very different things.

High speaking/listening, low reading/writing

Possible LTEL pattern: strong social language, weak academic literacy.
▶ Target academic vocabulary & content-area writing; don't mistake fluency for mastery.

Low across all four domains (recent arrival)

Newcomer/RAEL at early acquisition — a language barrier, not a learning barrier.
▶ Comprehensible input, native-language support, honor the silent period.

Low English AND low home-language literacy

Possible SIFE: foundational skill gaps beyond language.
▶ Build foundational literacy/numeracy alongside grade-level content; relationships first.

Stalled growth over several years

Student may be "stuck"; current supports aren't working.
▶ Re-examine instruction & engagement; set visible reclassification goals.

⑤ Avoiding Misidentification & Bias

The biggest errors come from treating ELs as one group or confusing a language difference with a disability or low ability (IRIS Center, n.d.).
