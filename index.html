Identifying & Supporting English Learners

A practical guide for understanding how English learners (ELs) are identified, distinguishing key EL subpopulations, and choosing classroom supports that fit each student. ELs are a heterogeneous group — this tool is built to help educators see the student, not a stereotype.

Grounded in IRIS Center, OELA, Colorín Colorado & the Arizona EL Handbook

How Are English Learners Identified?

In Arizona, identification follows a consistent legal process at enrollment, ensuring students who need language support are found and served (Arizona Department of Education [ADE], 2024).

1

Home Language Survey

At enrollment, every family completes the Home Language Survey (HLS). If a language other than English is indicated, the student is referred for screening.

→
2

Placement Screener (AZELLA)

The student takes the AZELLA placement test to measure English proficiency in listening, speaking, reading, and writing.

→
3

Classification & Placement

Results determine EL status and the appropriate language program. Families are notified and may consent to services.

→
4

Annual Reassessment

ELs take the AZELLA annually to monitor growth toward proficiency and eventual reclassification.

→
5

Reclassification & Monitoring

Once proficient, students are reclassified and monitored to confirm success in mainstream classes.

Know the Subpopulations

"English learner" is an umbrella term. These groups have very different histories and needs — click each to compare characteristics and the supports that fit.

LTEL

Long-Term English Learner

Who they are

  • Classified as an EL for 6+ years without reaching reclassification.
  • Typically U.S.-schooled, often since kindergarten.
  • Frequently sound fluent in conversational English.

Key characteristics & needs

  • Strong social language, but gaps in academic language & literacy.
  • Can be "invisible" — oral fluency masks the need (OELA, 2024).
  • May feel stuck, disengaged, or mislabeled over time.

Classroom supports & scaffolds

  • Explicit academic vocabulary & discipline-specific language instruction.
  • Structured reading/writing in the content areas; sentence frames.
  • Rigorous, grade-level tasks — not remediation that lowers expectations.
  • Goal-setting toward reclassification so progress is visible.

What proficiency data may suggest

  • High speaking/listening but lower reading/writing scores → target academic literacy, not basic communication.

RAEL

Recently Arrived English Learner (Newcomer)

Who they are

  • Enrolled in U.S. schools a short time (ESSA: under 12 months; often the first few years).
  • New to U.S. school culture, routines, and language.
  • May have strong, on-grade education in their home language.

Key characteristics & needs

  • Early stages of English; a possible silent period is normal.
  • Adjusting culturally and socially as well as linguistically.
  • Like Moisés in Immersion, may understand the content but not yet the language carrying it (EALTeam SIS, 2013).

Classroom supports & scaffolds

  • Comprehensible input: visuals, gestures, realia, demonstrations.
  • Native-language resources, bilingual partners, translation tools.
  • Allow non-verbal & drawn responses; honor the silent period.
  • Build on existing content knowledge from prior schooling.

What proficiency data may suggest

  • Low scores across all domains → focus first on access & comprehension; don't mistake a language barrier for low ability.

SIFE / SLIFE

Students with Interrupted (or Limited) Formal Education

Who they are

  • Significant gaps in prior schooling (often 2+ years).
  • May be below grade level in home-language literacy & numeracy.
  • Schooling interrupted by migration, conflict, or limited access.

Key characteristics & needs

  • Building English and foundational academic skills at once.
  • Unfamiliar with formal school expectations & routines.
  • Bring rich life experience & resilience as assets (Colorín Colorado, n.d.).

Classroom supports & scaffolds

  • Foundational literacy/numeracy alongside grade-level content.
  • Explicit teaching of school routines and "how to do school."
  • Highly scaffolded, relevant tasks; strong relationships & mentoring.
  • Avoid grade retention as a default; build on strengths.

What proficiency data may suggest

  • Low English and low home-language literacy → needs foundational skill-building, not just language acquisition.

Refugee Students

Special EL Population

Who they are

  • Resettled after fleeing conflict, persecution, or disaster.
  • Often arrive with interrupted schooling (may also be SIFE).
  • May have experienced trauma or time in refugee camps (Colorín Colorado, n.d.).

Key characteristics & needs

  • Social-emotional safety is a prerequisite for learning.
  • Adjusting to a new country, culture, and language simultaneously.
  • Families may need wraparound and community connection.

Classroom supports & scaffolds

  • Trauma-informed, predictable, welcoming classroom routines.
  • Connect families to school counselors & community resources.
  • Culturally responsive teaching that affirms students' identities.
  • Patience, relationship-building, and newcomer language supports.

What proficiency data may suggest

  • Read scores alongside background: low performance may reflect trauma or interrupted schooling, not ability or effort.

What Proficiency & Assessment Data Suggest

AZELLA reports growth across domains. The same overall score can mean very different things — always read the profile, not just the number, and pair it with what you know about the student.

Avoiding Misidentification & Bias

The biggest risks come from treating ELs as a single group or confusing a language difference with a disability or low ability (IRIS Center, n.d.).

⚠ Pitfall: Mistaking a language barrier for a learning disability

Referring ELs to special education because of slow progress in English, when the real issue is still-developing language.

✓ Avoid it

Distinguish language acquisition from disability; gather data over time and in the home language before referral. Difference ≠ disorder.

⚠ Pitfall: "Sounds fluent, must be fine"

Assuming conversational fluency equals academic readiness — the classic way LTELs get overlooked.

✓ Avoid it

Check academic literacy directly, not just oral fluency. Social language develops faster than academic language.

⚠ Pitfall: Deficit thinking & one-size-fits-all

Seeing ELs only for what they lack, or treating every EL the same regardless of background.

✓ Avoid it

Use an asset-based lens; honor home language and lived experience (Pang interview). Get to know each student's unique story.

⚠ Pitfall: Confusing quiet with not learning

Interpreting a newcomer's silent period as disengagement or low ability.

✓ Avoid it

Expect and allow a silent period; provide low-anxiety ways to show understanding (drawing, pointing, native language).
